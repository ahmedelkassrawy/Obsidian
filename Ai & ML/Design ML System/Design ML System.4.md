# Training Data in Machine Learning

Training data is a fundamental component of machine learning (ML) systems, crucial for developing and improving ML models. Its importance often goes underestimated, and managing it can be complex and challenging, leading to significant time and effort for data scientists and ML engineers. ML models are heavily skewed towards well-managed and high-quality training data.

## I. Importance and Challenges of Training Data

- **Crucial for Development**: Training data forms the foundation for developing and improving ML models.
- **Complex and Treacherous**: Managing training data is a messy, complex, and potentially treacherous task. If not handled properly, it can significantly impact the entire ML operation.
- **Data Distribution Shifts**: The term "training data" refers to a static dataset, but in practice, data distributions can shift over time, making training data an iterative process.
- **Bias**: Data collection, sampling, and labeling processes can introduce biases, which are often hidden and difficult to detect. Human biases can also inadvertently influence training data.

## II. Sampling

Sampling is an integral part of the ML workflow that is often overlooked in typical ML coursework. It involves selecting a subset of real-world data to create datasets for training, validation, and testing purposes.

### A. Families of Sampling

The sources mention two families of sampling: **nonprobability sampling** and **probability sampling**.

### B. Nonprobability Sampling

This type of sampling occurs when data selection is not based on any probability criteria.

- **Convenience Sampling**: Samples are selected based on their availability, often popular due to its convenience.
- **Snowball Sampling**: Future samples are selected based on existing samples, often used to scrape legitimate data from large databases like Twitter.
- **Judgment Sampling**: Experts decide what samples to include.
- **Quota Sampling**: Samples are chosen based on quotas for certain slices of data without strict randomization.

### C. Probability Sampling

This type of sampling involves selecting data where each element has a known probability of being chosen.

- **Simple Random Sampling**: The simplest form, where all samples in the population have an equal chance of being selected. A drawback is that rare categories might not appear in the selection.
- **Stratified Sampling**: Divides the population into groups (strata) and then samples from each group to ensure representation, especially for rare classes.
- **Weighted Sampling**: Assigns a weight to each sample, influencing its probability of being selected. This method can leverage domain expertise and allows for a higher chance of selecting more valuable data.
- **Reservoir Sampling**: A fascinating algorithm for streaming data, particularly useful when you don't know the total number of items beforehand. It ensures that every item has an equal probability of being selected into the reservoir.

## III. Labeling

Labeling is the process of annotating data to provide the "ground truth" for ML models to learn from. It's a critical but often underestimated step in production ML systems.

### A. Challenges in Labeling

- **Underspecified Labels**: Despite their importance, ML models often depend heavily on the quality and quantity of labeled data.
- **Data Lineage**: It's crucial to understand the origin of data and its labels, as well as who annotated them, to avoid mysterious model failures.
- **Label Multiplicity**: Disagreements among annotators are common, leading to higher potential for annotation disagreement. This problem results in different levels of expertise and can lead to label ambiguity or conflicts. Resolving these conflicts requires clear definitions and sometimes re-labeling.

### B. Types of Labels

- **Hand Labels**: Manually created by human annotators, often expensive and time-consuming. Privacy concerns and legal requirements can limit the use of certain data for hand labeling.
- **Natural Labels**: These are typically based on observable user behavior, such as a user clicking on a recommended item, or explicit feedback like a thumbs-up.
- **Programmatic Labels**: Generated by heuristics or rules. These are fast but can be less accurate than hand labels.
- **Implicit Labels**: Often derived when a negative outcome is assumed if a positive outcome does not occur (e.g., if a user doesn't click a link, it's implicitly negative).
- **Feedback Loop Labels**: Labels derived from a model's own predictions, creating a feedback loop. This can be short-term (e.g., immediate clicks) or long-term (e.g., purchases or revenue).

## IV. Handling the Lack of Labels

Due to challenges in acquiring sufficient high-quality labels, various techniques have been developed.

- **Weak Supervision**: Leverages heuristics or programmatic rules to generate labels, especially useful when hand labels are scarce. It's a powerful paradigm but requires careful application.
- **Semi-supervision**: Uses a small initial set of labeled data along with a large amount of unlabeled data to generate more labels. It can improve performance with less labeled data than fully supervised methods.
- **Transfer Learning**: Involves using a model pre-trained on a different, often larger, dataset or task, and then fine-tuning it for the target task with limited labeled data. This is effective when the target task has few labels or when starting from scratch would require massive data.
- **Active Learning**: An iterative process where the ML model actively queries a human annotator for labels on specific data samples that are most informative for its training. This helps improve accuracy with fewer labels by prioritizing ambiguous or uncertain data points.
- **Data Augmentation**: Increases the amount of training data by creating new, modified versions of existing data. This can involve simple label-preserving transformations (e.g., cropping, rotating images) or more complex perturbation techniques (e.g., adding noise) to improve model robustness.
- **Data Synthesis**: Generates entirely new training data, often used in computer vision and NLP. This can involve creating synthetic images or generating new conversational remarks based on templates.

## V. Class Imbalance

Class imbalance occurs when one class significantly outnumbers other classes in the training data. This can severely impact model performance, especially for the minority class, leading to models that favor the majority class.

### A. Challenges

- **Misleading Accuracy**: Accuracy can be a poor metric for imbalanced datasets because a high accuracy might simply reflect the model predicting the majority class correctly most of the time, while performing poorly on the minority class.
- **Difficulty in Detection**: It can be difficult to detect class imbalance without proper analysis of the data.

### B. Evaluation Metrics

To better evaluate models on imbalanced datasets, alternative metrics are used:

- **Precision**: The proportion of true positive predictions among all positive predictions.
- **Recall (Sensitivity/True Positive Rate)**: The proportion of true positive predictions among all actual positive instances.
- **F1 Score**: The harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balanced measure of a model's accuracy on the positive class.
- **ROC Curve**: Plots the true positive rate against the false positive rate, useful for visualizing model performance across different thresholds, especially for imbalanced data.

### C. Methods to Handle Class Imbalance

- **Data-Level Methods (Resampling)**:
    - **Undersampling**: Reduces the number of instances in the majority class to balance the dataset.
    - **Oversampling**: Increases the number of instances in the minority class, often by duplicating or synthesizing new samples (e.g., SMOTE).
    - These techniques aim to reduce the level of imbalance in the training data.
- **Algorithm-Level Methods**:
    - **Cost-Sensitive Learning**: Modifies the loss function to penalize misclassifications of the minority class more heavily than the majority class, making the model more sensitive to errors on the minority class.
    - **Class-Balanced Loss**: Aims to balance the weight of each class inversely proportional to the number of samples in that class.
    - **Focal Loss**: A more sophisticated version of class-balanced loss, which reduces the relative loss for well-classified examples, putting more focus on hard, misclassified examples.
